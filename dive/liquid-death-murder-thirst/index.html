How Liquid Death sold canned tap water by pretending to kill you.

Zero to a $1.4 billion valuation in five years. Same water as everyone else's. The only thing they changed was the part of your brain it talked to.

Here's the entire pitch. The whole thing. The reason this brand is worth a billion dollars:

What if water, but punk?

That's it. That's the strategy doc. Mike Cessario, the founder, used to direct music videos for heavy metal bands. He was at Warped Tour in 2009, where the bottled water sponsor was Monster Energy, and he watched touring musicians walk on stage holding cans of Monster knowing it was disgusting just so they wouldn't look uncool with a bottle of Smartwater. And he had the thought that builds billion-dollar companies, which is: this is dumb. I should fix this.

So in 2017 he bought the domain liquiddeath.com for nine dollars, made a fake Facebook ad for a brand that didn't exist yet, and the ad went viral. Three million views, no product. He raised a $1.6M seed round on the strength of a Facebook ad for water that wasn't real. Then he made the water real.

A guy who made music videos walks into a VC office holding a fake ad for fake water and asks for $1.6M. The VCs say yes. This is the moment in the simulation when you're supposed to question reality.

01The Campaign

Liquid Death's "campaign" isn't a single ad. It's a posture. The brand voice is set so aggressively that every asset is a campaign. The headline that actually launched the company was on the original 2017 Facebook ad and it just said:

"Murder your thirst."

Three words. They built a billion-dollar valuation on three words. To be fair, those words are doing an absurd amount of work. We'll come back to that.

The product launched on Amazon in 2019 as a 16.9 oz tallboy can of mountain water, sourced from an aquifer in the Austrian Alps because that's a sentence a heavy-metal water brand has to be able to say. The can looked like a Pabst Blue Ribbon designed by Iron Maiden. It was placed in retail next to alcohol and energy drinks, never water. By 2024 they were in 113,000 stores. Whole Foods. 7-11. Walmart. Sheetz.

Then the "campaigns" started. Not in any traditional ad-agency sense. Stunts:

  • → Tony Hawk's Tony Hawk (2021): cans painted with Tony Hawk's actual blood. Sold 100 cans for $500 each. Sold out in minutes.
  • → "Plastic Surgery" with Steve-O (2022): a real surgical procedure where Steve-O had liquid death "injected" into him, parodying influencer plastic surgery culture.
  • → "The Dehydrator" (2023): a horror-movie-style ad where a college kid is murdered for drinking from a plastic water bottle.
  • → Super Bowl 2024: their first national TV spot was an ad pretending to be an ad for an energy drink, then ripping it apart.

02The Spend

Here's where it gets stupid in a fun way. Liquid Death's total marketing spend across five years is roughly $45 million. That's an estimate; they don't disclose. It's derived from the funding rounds and what fraction of those rounds typically goes to marketing for a CPG brand. Even being generous, double that. Call it $90M. Doesn't matter. Look at it next to the people they were beating:

How do you build a billion-dollar brand at 1/250th the budget of the leader? You don't outspend them. You out-weird them. The category was so visually identical (every water brand uses white, blue, and the word "pure") that the cost of getting noticed was effectively zero, because nobody was trying.

If you ever launch in a category where every competitor's logo could be swapped without anyone noticing, you have my permission to be aggressively, gloriously stupid. It's free attention.

Notice what's missing. Zero print ads. Almost no out-of-home. No traditional banner display. The entire brand is built on Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and the can itself. The can is the campaign. Every time someone holds it at a festival, that's a ten-second TV spot.

04Why It Worked

Bottled water is what economists call a commodity, which is a fancy way of saying nobody can tell the difference between any of it. In a blind taste test, Voss, Aquafina, Liquid Death, and the tap water in your apartment all score the same. The only thing brands can compete on is what the package means.

So the entire bottled water aisle made the same choice. They all decided to mean "health." White packaging, blue accents, words like pure, crisp, essential, artesian. The aisle looks like a hospital. Every brand is fighting over the same few customers who walked in already wanting to feel virtuous.

Liquid Death asked one question and the answer is the entire reason they are now worth more than most regional banks:

"What if we mean the opposite of every other water on this shelf?"

Three water brands walk into a Coachella. Two of them die of dehydration trying to be classy. Liquid Death sells out by 2pm.

Their whole strategy is recognition through revolt. In a category where everyone is whispering, the brand that screams owns the entire conversation, even if the screaming costs $22M and the competitors are spending $5B.

05What Specifically Worked

Most "great strategy" stories collapse when you ask what specifically worked. Liquid Death is unusually easy to dissect because they made about four decisions and every one of them is load-bearing.

1. The can, not the bottle. A tallboy can looks like a beer at a music festival. A plastic bottle looks like a hospital prop. People who would feel embarrassed holding a Smartwater at a concert feel cool holding a Liquid Death. Same molecule of water. 100% different social signal.

2. The slogan. "Murder Your Thirst" is doing two jobs at once. The 14-year-old reads it as cool because it says murder. The 38-year-old reads it as funny because it's ironic. One slogan, two audiences, both think it's for them.

3. The "Death To Plastic" tagline. This is the move that turns the brand from a joke into a defensible business. The can has an actual environmental argument (aluminum recycles infinitely; PET plastic doesn't). It gives the brand a moral reason to exist beyond aesthetic. Now your weird heavy-metal water is also virtuous. You can buy it for the joke and feel good about it.

4. The typography. Sui Generis Open Cap. The can looks like an Iron Maiden album cover printed on a Pabst Blue Ribbon. Recognizable from across a parking lot. You don't even need to read the label to know what it is.

Out of these four, the one nobody talks about is the typography. It's doing as much work as the slogan. If they had set it in Helvetica, this brand wouldn't exist.

06The Audience

Liquid Death's actual target wasn't "Gen Z." That's lazy demographic talk. Their target was specific and weird:

People who want to drink water but feel uncool doing it.

Festival kids who don't want to be holding a Smartwater on Instagram. Sober-curious 20-somethings who quit drinking but still want to look like they're at the bar. Tattoo artists. Skaters. Stand-up comics. Vegans (this one is funnier than you'd think — Liquid Death is wildly popular with the vegan/straight-edge crowd because the brand reads as "uncompromising"). Mothers who want to be cool to their teenagers.

None of those groups are big alone. Together they're a market. And every one of them was being ignored by the Smartwaters of the world, who were optimizing for the soccer-mom-with-a-yoga-mat customer.

07The Targeting

The targeting strategy is the part that doesn't get enough credit because it sounds boring. It isn't.

Festival sampling. 2019 to 2022, they were free at every major US music festival. Coachella, Lollapalooza, Bonnaroo, Warped Tour's revival. They didn't sell, they gave away, by the pallet, and the trade was: everyone holding our can on Instagram for three days is our marketing.

TikTok organic, not paid. Their TikTok feels like a metal band's account. They're shitposting. They reply to commenters in character. They've trained their audience to expect it, which means the audience itself becomes the campaign machinery.

Influencers nobody else picks. Steve-O. Tony Hawk. Bert Kreischer. Whitney Cummings. Drag queens. People with massive followings who would never be touched by Aquafina because their personal brand is "wrong." Liquid Death's whole thesis is that the wrong influencer is exactly the right one.

Retail placement. They paid extra to be shelved next to beer and energy drinks, not water. This is the most expensive choice in the strategy and the one most often skipped by case-study writers. The shelf placement is the campaign. A 7-11 customer reaching for a Monster sees a Liquid Death right next to it. They don't see Smartwater because Smartwater is in the boring aisle three rows over.

The strategy isn't on the can. It's where the can sits.

The verdict

Liquid Death proved that in any commodity category, the marketing problem is bigger than the product problem. Their water is fine. So is everyone else's. They won by treating bottled water as a category that needed subversion, not optimization.

The lesson, if you're trying to take one home: when every player in your category is making the same five choices, the cheapest path to a billion dollars is making the opposite of those five choices, and being willing to look stupid until it works.

Mike Cessario looked very stupid for two years. Then he looked very rich.
