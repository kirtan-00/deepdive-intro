How three words built Nike into a $190 billion religion.

"Just Do It." was written in a basement in Portland in 1988. The line was inspired by the last words of a death-row inmate. It was supposed to last one campaign cycle. It is now the most quoted sentence in advertising history.

The most valuable sentence in marketing history was inspired by a guy who shot two people in cold blood.

Gary Gilmore was executed by firing squad in Utah on January 17, 1977. They asked him for last words. He said: "Let's do it."

Eleven years later, in 1988, Dan Wieden was sitting in a Portland conference room trying to write a tagline for Nike's new umbrella campaign. The brief was a mess. Nike was about to launch eight different ads at once across eight different sports, with eight different audiences, and the agency had no idea how to glue them together. The night before the pitch, Wieden was tired and remembered Gilmore.

"He was such a piece of shit, and yet he just kept on with absolute determination. The whole thing was: action."

Wieden changed one word. Just instead of Let's. "Just Do It." He wrote it on a napkin. Nike's leadership hated it. Phil Knight's exact words, according to Wieden himself, were: "We don't need that shit."

They ran it anyway. The campaign launched in July 1988. Nike's market share in the U.S. athletic shoe market at that time: 18%. Within ten years it would be 43%. The line is still running. It's older than most of the people who quote it.

An ad guy hears a murderer's last words, scribbles three words on a napkin, the client says "we don't need that shit," and 36 years later the line is on a billion T-shirts. The advertising industry, ladies and gentlemen.

01The Campaigns

"Just Do It" isn't one campaign. It's a chassis that Nike has dropped a new engine into every few years for almost four decades. Each chapter solved a different audience problem with the same three words at the bottom.

  • '88Walt Stack, 80, runs across Golden Gate Bridge. The first JDI ad. They didn't pick a young athlete. They picked an 80-year-old man jogging across a bridge in his sweatpants. The thesis: "athlete" is anyone who shows up. This single casting decision unlocked the next $180B.
  • '89Bo Knows. Bo Jackson playing every sport, badly, until everyone admits he's good at all of them. Cross-category permission. The line "Bo knows ___" became syntax.
  • '95If You Let Me Play. Young girls listing the statistics of women's sports participation. The first major ad to treat girls' athletics as a moral question, not a market segment. Nike Women became a $9B/year business off the back of it.
  • '97Failure / Michael Jordan. Jordan reads his own résumé of misses. "I've missed more than 9,000 shots. I've lost almost 300 games." Reframed failure as the path, not the obstacle. Most-watched basketball ad of the decade.
  • '08Find Your Greatness. Olympic-year campaign filmed entirely in towns called London — except London, England. Suburban Ohio. Rural Jamaica. Reframed the Olympics as something happening on your own street.
  • '18Dream Crazy / Kaepernick. "Believe in something. Even if it means sacrificing everything." Nike risked $4B in market cap on a single 30th-anniversary spot. Stock dropped 3% that morning, recovered by the end of the week, finished the quarter at an all-time high.
  • '20You Can't Stop Us. 36-second split-screen pandemic spot. Two athletes per shot, mirrored across the seam, every frame matched to the millisecond. Won every award in advertising. JDI sat at the bottom of the screen the whole time.

Seven campaigns spanning 36 years. Different sports, different politics, different audiences, different decades. Same three words at the end.

02The Spend

Nike has spent more on marketing than most countries spend on healthcare. Their cumulative ad spend since 1988 is somewhere north of $80 billion. Their annual spend is now $4.1B, more than any other apparel brand by a wide margin. But here's the part that matters:

The lesson buried in this chart isn't "outspend the competition." Anybody can do that with enough money. The lesson is that they kept the same line through every CEO, every recession, every category pivot, for thirty-six years. Most brands change tagline every two CMOs. Nike never did. Compounding interest applies to slogans too.

If you switch lines every three years, your brand has the long-term recognition of a Hinge profile.

04Why It Worked

Most ad copy tries to convince you the brand is great. "Just Do It" doesn't mention Nike at all. It doesn't mention shoes, sports, or any product feature. Read the line again:

Just. Do. It.

Notice who the line is about. You. The reader. The line is an instruction directed at the customer, not a description of the brand. Nike effectively spent $80 billion buying the right to be the brand standing next to a sentence about your potential. Anytime you needed permission to start something, the brand was already there pre-loaded with the answer.

This is the whole trick. Most slogans are about the company. JDI is about the customer. It's a permission slip, not a product claim. You can't outgrow it because you can't outgrow needing permission to act.

05What Specifically Worked

Three words. Each one is loadbearing.

"Just" — the most underrated word in the line. It implies the action is small. It removes the moral weight. It says: this isn't a big deal. Don't overthink it. Don't research it. Don't make a vision board about it. Just.

"Do" — imperative verb. Direct address. Most ad copy uses descriptive verbs ("Refresh your day," "Discover the difference"). JDI uses the bossiest verb in English. It is unmistakably an instruction.

"It" — and this is the genius. The line never names the action. "It" is whatever you bring to the line. Running. Quitting your job. Calling your mom. Asking her out. The slogan is a glass that takes the shape of whatever ambition you pour into it.

If they had written "Just do something," the line would be dead. The pronoun is the trick.

Add to this the supporting cast: the Walt Stack casting choice (anyone is an athlete), the Jordan failure ad (failing is the path), the Kaepernick line (sacrifice for what you believe in). Each campaign attaches a slightly different "It" to the line. Over 36 years, "It" has come to mean: action, persistence, defiance, courage, ambition, return. The slogan is now a Rorschach test that always benefits the brand.

06The Audience

Here is the part of the Nike story most marketing books get wrong. JDI was not aimed at athletes. It was aimed at people who feel like impostors at the gym.

The brief in 1988 was that Nike was losing badly to Reebok in the women's aerobics market. Reebok had cracked the housewife-doing-Jane-Fonda audience, and Nike, which was selling to college sprinters, looked dorky and intimidating. Nike's research showed that most Americans didn't go to the gym not because they didn't want to, but because they felt unworthy of being there. They thought the gym was for athletes, and they weren't athletes.

JDI was a piece of psychological reframing. By making "athlete" mean anyone who tries, Nike turned the entire population of gym-avoiders into a market. The Walt Stack ad was the proof of concept. An 80-year-old in sweatpants is the audience, not an exception.

Nike's target market wasn't athletes. It was the 90% of people who thought they couldn't be one.

07The Targeting

Nike's targeting has evolved through three eras, and each one rhymed with the same playbook.

1988–1998: Endorsement as proof. Sign every era-defining athlete you can. Jordan. Bo. Tiger. Federer. Use them as social proof, not as product testers. The athlete makes the brand legitimate. Then the brand sells to the spectator.

1998–2015: Mass democratization. Olympics. World Cups. Halftime shows. National TV. The line moves from sports culture into culture. Now your dentist quotes JDI. The brand is no longer for athletes; it's for everyone with a body.

2015–today: Cultural stance. Kaepernick. Equality. Until We All Win. Nike starts associating the line with social positions, knowing they will lose part of the audience and gain a more loyal one. The line moves from gym to politics. A brand that picks fights becomes a brand that gets defended.

Each era is the previous era plus a layer. The line keeps absorbing new meaning without ever changing.

The verdict

"Just Do It" is the most-quoted sentence in advertising history because it does the one thing slogans almost never do: it stops being about the brand. Nike spent four decades and $80 billion buying the right to stand next to a sentence about your potential, and now you can't think the sentence without thinking of them.

The lesson, sweaty and honest: brands that last write copy about the customer, not themselves. Brands that write copy about themselves change tagline every three years and slowly disappear. Pick a customer-side instruction so universal that it never expires, then stand next to it for thirty-six years and don't blink.

Also, sometimes the most valuable line in marketing history starts with a death-row inmate. Take notes.
